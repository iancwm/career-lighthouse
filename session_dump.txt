## Q2
What's the difference between an investment bank and the bank where I have my checking account?

### A2
Great foundational question! These are actually very different types of institutions, even though both have "bank" in the name.

---

### Your Checking Account Bank — **Retail/Commercial Bank**

This is what most people interact with daily. Examples: **DBS, OCBC, UOB** in Singapore.

What they do:
- Hold deposits (your savings/checking account)
- Give out loans (mortgages, personal loans, car loans)
- Offer credit cards
- Make money on the **difference between deposit interest rates and lending rates**

---

### Investment Banks — A Very Different Business

Examples from my knowledge base: **Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan, Citi, UBS**

What they do:
- Help **companies raise money** (issuing stocks or bonds)
- Advise on **mergers and acquisitions (M&A)** — when companies buy or merge with each other
- Trade financial instruments (equities, bonds, derivatives)
- Manage large institutional investments

They work with **corporations and governments**, not everyday consumers.

---

### A Simple Analogy

| | Retail Bank | Investment Bank |
|---|---|---|
| **Clients** | Individuals | Companies, governments |
| **Services** | Deposits, loans | M&A advice, fundraising, trading |
| **Everyday example** | Your DBS savings account | Advising Grab on its IPO |

---

### Why This Matters for Your Career

Based on my knowledge base, **investment banking (IBD)** at firms like Goldman Sachs is one of SMU's most competitive career paths:

- GS Singapore hires **4-6 IBD analysts/year** from SMU
- Requires strong GPA (3.7+), finance knowledge, and interview preparation including **DCF, LBO, and M&A concepts**
- SMU Investment Club is a key stepping stone

## Q3
What does a 'Consultant' actually do all day? Do they just give advice?

### A3
It's a fair question — "giving advice" sounds vague! The reality is more structured and demanding than that.

---

### The Core Job

Consultants are hired by companies (or governments) to **solve specific business problems** that the client either doesn't have the expertise or bandwidth to solve internally.

Think of it like this: a company's CEO knows something is broken — maybe they're losing market share, or they want to expand into Vietnam, or they need to cut costs. They hire consultants to **diagnose the problem and recommend solutions**.

---

### What a Typical Day Actually Looks Like

| Activity | What It Involves |
|---|---|
| **Structuring the problem** | Breaking a big messy question into smaller, answerable parts |
| **Data gathering** | Interviews, surveys, market research, financial analysis |
| **Excel/PowerPoint work** | A *lot* of this — building models, making slides |
| **Client meetings** | Presenting findings, asking questions, managing relationships |
| **Internal team discussions** | Debating hypotheses, checking each other's logic |

So no — it's not just giving advice. There's significant **analytical and communication work** behind every recommendation.

---

### A Real Example

Imagine a Singaporean bank hires McKinsey and asks: *"Should we expand into Indonesia?"*

The consulting team would:
1. Analyze the Indonesian banking market size
2. Study competitors already there
3. Model the financial returns
4. Identify risks
5. Present a structured recommendation with evidence

*(This type of work is exactly what McKinsey Singapore does — my knowledge base notes they do significant **government and GLC work** in Singapore too)*

---

### The Honest Reality

- **Junior consultants (analysts)** spend more time on data gathering and slides than client-facing work
- **Travel** can be significant — you may be at a client's office frequently
- **Hours are long**, especially before major client presentations
- The learning curve is steep but fast

---

### Why SMU Candidates Are Well-Positioned

Based on **consulting-paths.txt**, SMU has some specific advantages:

> *"SMU's seminar format trains you to articulate under pressure — use that in your interviews."*

The ability to **structure your thinking and communicate clearly under pressure** is exactly what consulting demands daily. SMU's interactive classroom style genuinely builds this.

MBB firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain) hire **10-12 SMU students per year combined**, which is meaningful for a Singapore-based recruiting pool.

## Q4
I keep seeing roles for 'Analysts' and 'Associates.' Which one is higher up?

### A4
Great question, and the answer can be **confusing because it depends on the industry**. Let me break it down.

---

### General Rule: Associate is Higher Than Analyst

In most corporate career tracks:

| Level | Typical Profile |
|---|---|
| **Analyst** | Fresh graduate (entry-level) |
| **Associate** | ~2-4 years experience, or MBA hire |
| **Manager/VP/Director** | More senior still |

Think of it as: **Analyst → Associate → Manager → VP → Director → MD/Partner**

---

### How This Plays Out in Industries Relevant to SMU

#### Investment Banking (e.g., Goldman Sachs)
- **Analyst**: Fresh graduate, 2-3 year program. This is what SMU students recruit into.
- **Associate**: Typically post-MBA or promoted Analyst. More client-facing responsibility.

From **goldman-singapore-guide.txt**, GS Singapore hires SMU students as **Analysts** (4-6 IBD analysts/year). That's your entry point.

#### Consulting (e.g., McKinsey, Bain, BCG)
- **Analyst/Business Analyst**: Undergraduate entry-level hire
- **Associate/Consultant**: Typically post-MBA or promoted Analyst

From **consulting-paths.txt**, SMU graduates enter MBB at the **Analyst level**, with applications opening August and deadline in October for full-time roles.

#### GIC / Public Sector
- Titles vary more — GIC uses terms like **Investment Analyst** at the graduate entry level

From **gic-recruiting-guide.txt**, fresh SMU graduates enter the Investment Track as entry-level hires through the August application cycle.

---

### A Simple Way to Remember

> **Analyst = where you start**
> **Associate = where you go after proving yourself**

When you see a job posting as a fresh graduate or final-year student, you should almost always be targeting **Analyst-level roles**.

---

### One Caveat

Some companies (especially tech firms) use these titles differently — a "Senior Analyst" could outrank a junior "Associate." Always check the **job description and reporting structure**, not just the title.

## Q9
What is an 'off-cycle' internship, and why are people taking a Leave of Absence (LOA) for it? Won't I graduate later than my friends?

### A9
This is a very real phenomenon at SMU, and worth understanding clearly before you decide whether it's right for you.

---

### What Is an "Off-Cycle" Internship?

Most internships you hear about are **on-cycle** — structured programs that run during a fixed summer window (typically May–August), recruited for months in advance with a formal campus process.

**Off-cycle internships** are everything outside that window:
- Roles that start in **January, March, September, October** — any non-summer period
- Often less structured, shorter notice, filled on a **rolling/ad-hoc basis**
- Common at: **banks, private equity firms, hedge funds, boutique advisories**

They exist because deals don't stop in September just because recruiting season ended.

---

### Why Do People Take LOAs for Them?

Off-cycle internships often run **4–6 months**, sometimes longer. You cannot do a full-time internship in London, Hong Kong, or even Singapore while attending SMU classes.

So students take a **Leave of Absence** — officially pausing their studies for one or two semesters — to do the internship, then return to complete their degree.

#### Why would someone do this?

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Access to roles that don't recruit on-campus** | Many PE firms, hedge funds, and boutique banks in Singapore and Hong Kong **only hire off-cycle** |
| **Depth of experience** | 4-6 months gives you real deal exposure vs. 10-week summer programs |
| **Competitive differentiation** | An off-cycle stint at a top firm signals serious commitment |
| **Internship conversion** | Some firms use off-cycle internships as **the primary pipeline for full-time hires** |

---

### Is It Worth Graduating Later?

#### Reasons It Can Be Worth It
- The internship-to-full-time conversion rate at GS Singapore is **80%** — the internship *is* effectively the hiring process
- In competitive finance tracks, **what firm you interned at often matters more than when you graduated**
- One extra semester is a relatively small cost against a 30-year career trajectory

#### Reasons to Think Carefully
- **Financial cost**: One extra semester of tuition and living expenses
- **Social dimension**: Yes, your cohort moves on — this is real
- **Not always necessary**: For GS, GIC, and MBB, the on-cycle SMU recruiting path is well-established. You don't *need* an LOA to access these firms.
- **Opportunity cost**: That semester could be used for finals, CFA prep, or networking for full-time roles

---

### When Does an LOA Make Most Sense for SMU Students?

1. **You're targeting firms with no SMU on-campus presence** — boutique PE, hedge funds, family offices, Hong Kong or regional roles
2. **You received an off-cycle offer at a genuinely prestigious firm** and the on-cycle window has passed
3. **You want to explore a path before committing** — some students use an LOA internship to confirm whether IBD or PE is actually right for them

It makes **less sense** if you're using it to chase prestige without a clear offer, or if the firm is accessible through normal SMU recruiting channels anyway.

---

### A Practical Note on SMU's LOA Process

SMU does allow LOAs for internship purposes — speak to your **academic advisor and SMU Career Services together** before deciding. Key questions:

- Does this affect my financial aid or scholarship conditions?
- Can I return to the same academic cohort?
- Will this affect my CAP calculation?
